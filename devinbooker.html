Devin Booker

Devin Booker is an active NBA player for the Phoenix Suns. In 2021, he made the postseason for the first time, leading his team to a Western Conference championship and Finals appearance. During that postseason, he scored 601 points, an all-time record for points scored in a player's first postseason. I wanted to break down the unforgettable performance by-the-numbers in an infographic, giving context into the greatness behind his efforts.

Visual Identity

This infographic focuses on highlighting the statistic of 601 points and dissecting it in different ways that showcase how those points were scored. To prevent confusion, hierarchy and proper labelling of charts and graphs was imperative. This would separate data sets and make it clear to the viewer exactly what each data point represented. I also wanted to strategically incorporate imagery around the data in place of team and location names, leaving text for numerical purposes.

Typography

Looking for a typeface that was bold and could be displayed in both regular and italicized styles, I chose "Big Caslon CC" to use throughout the entire infographic. Its alphabetical and numerical characters are formed by strokes that fluidly change widths, most of which end with pointed serifs.

The color scheme matches that of the Suns' organization. The purple and orange work well to visually represent data, while white and black text and overlays give a nice contrast for readability and separation. The tan color is meant to bring a desert feel to the entire piece, consistent with Phoenix, Arizona, where the Suns play.

Infographic

An image of the Phoenix desert sets the tone for the infographic. A horizontal bar chart showing game-by-game scoring detail is split into sections by playoff-series separating data by opponent. To subtly represent those opponents, their logos are incorporated in a faded-manner atop each section of bars. On the right side of the page, a pie-chart divides the 601 points by shot type, offering a perspective of where shots were made from. The chart is placed over the basketball in the player image, using its circular shape to give the impression that the player is shooting the chart.
